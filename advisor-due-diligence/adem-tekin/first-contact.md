# First contact — Adem Tekin

## Contact principle

The first message should ask for **research advice**, not admission, supervision, funding, or a recommendation. It should show that the candidate has read two specific works and can state one bounded intersection between the group’s active science and their engineering background.

**Evidence basis:** PNcsp+ is public Python research software for interpretable CSP with ML-potential ranking, and the group’s 2025 perovskite work combines DFT datasets with ML screening and explicit validation. [PNcsp+](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC13085239/) · [perovskite study](https://pubs.rsc.org/en/content/articlepdf/2025/cp/d4cp04218b)

## Recommended email

**Subject:** Advice on reproducible crystal-structure prediction workflows

> Dear Professor Tekin,
>
> I am a senior software engineer with more than ten years of industry experience, now deliberately preparing for research at the intersection of scientific software engineering, computational materials science, and AI-assisted materials discovery.
>
> I recently read your PNcsp+ paper and the group’s work on machine-learning-assisted screening of lead-free mixed-cation perovskites. I was particularly interested in the way the work combines an interpretable chemical representation, data-driven ranking, and later computational validation rather than treating ML as an isolated prediction task.
>
> My background is in software architecture and open-source development. I am also building a small project called Materials Atlas, focused on making materials knowledge and evidence more transparent and traceable. I would like to learn where practices such as reproducible workflows, benchmark design, provenance tracking, testing, and documentation can make a genuinely scientific contribution to current crystal-structure-prediction or materials-screening work.
>
> I am not writing to ask about admission at this stage. If you would be open to it, I would be very grateful for a short discussion about which research directions or preparation steps you would recommend for someone making this transition.
>
> I would be happy to send a concise one-page background note and links to my work in advance. Thank you for your time and consideration.
>
> Kind regards,<br>
> [Full name]<br>
> [LinkedIn / GitHub / personal site]<br>
> [Materials Atlas link, only if public and polished]

## Why this message works

| Element | Purpose |
| --- | --- |
| Names two actual papers | Signals preparation without flattery or citation dumping. |
| Describes the scientific pattern | Shows the candidate understood the research contribution—not merely keywords like “AI” or “Python.” |
| Defines software as a scientific enabler | Makes the senior-engineering background relevant without claiming to replace materials expertise. |
| Mentions Materials Atlas once | Keeps it natural, as evidence of provenance/research-data thinking, rather than pitching an unrelated product. |
| Explicitly excludes admission request | Gives the advisor a low-pressure reason to reply. |
| Asks for advice and preparation | Invites an academically useful response even if no opening exists. |

## Send only after this pre-send check

- [ ] Read PNcsp+ and the 2025 perovskite paper enough to answer a follow-up question.
- [ ] Replace every bracketed placeholder.
- [ ] Add at most two stable links; do not attach a long CV, slide deck, or repository dump.
- [ ] Make sure Materials Atlas has a public README/landing page if linked.
- [ ] Remove any sentence implying “please accept me,” “guarantee funding,” or “I need a supervisor.”
- [ ] Keep it below 230 words.

## If the advisor replies positively

Reply within one business day with:

1. three time windows, including one short option;
2. a one-page background note (not a 10-page portfolio);
3. the research question you hope to discuss: *where reproducibility/benchmark/provenance work could change a scientific result in the group’s current CSP pipeline*;
4. confirmation that the conversation is advice-seeking and exploratory.

Do **not** send a thesis proposal before hearing which active problem matters to the group.

## If the advisor asks for a CV

Send a two-page research-transition CV with this ordering:

1. a 3-line research objective;
2. selected software systems/open-source work showing quality, scale and ownership;
3. Materials Atlas with a one-line scientific-software relevance statement;
4. education and current scientific-computing learning;
5. a short “preparation in progress” list: Python, Linux/HPC, DFT/CSP reading, reproducibility work;
6. no inflated materials claim and no irrelevant enterprise project inventory.

## If there is no response

Wait 10–14 calendar days, then send one follow-up of no more than 70 words:

> Dear Professor Tekin,<br>
> I am following up on my brief note about reproducible CSP and materials-screening workflows. I understand you may be busy; if a discussion is not feasible, I would still appreciate any suggestion of one paper, project direction, or preparation step to pursue. Thank you again for your time.<br>
> Kind regards,<br>
> [Name]

Do not send a third follow-up. A lack of reply is not evidence of poor supervision; it is simply not enough evidence to make a relationship decision.

## Conversation strategy after the email

### First five minutes

Start from the work, not from biography:

> “I was drawn to PNcsp+ because it makes a chemical-similarity assumption visible and then uses ML potentials as a ranking layer. I am interested in where reproducibility or benchmark work would reveal scientifically important failures in that workflow.”

Then introduce the candidate background in one sentence:

> “My contribution is usually turning a complex workflow into something repeatable, inspectable and maintainable; I want to learn enough computational materials science to make that work answer a real research question.”

### How to introduce Materials Atlas

> “Materials Atlas is an experiment in source-linked, transparent materials knowledge. I do not assume it should be part of your group’s work. I am curious whether a very small provenance record for a benchmark or predicted-candidate set would solve a real problem for you, or whether I should keep it separate.”

This framing gives the advisor permission to say no. That is useful information.

### How to introduce Research Landscape

> “I also build structured research-information systems. It has shaped how I think about traceability and metadata, but I see it as background rather than a project I would expect you to supervise.”

Do not spend more than 20 seconds on it unless the advisor asks.

### How to ask for guidance rather than a place

> “What would you recommend I reproduce, read, or build over the next few months to learn whether I can make a useful contribution in this space?”

End with:

> “If I do that work and the fit becomes clearer, would it be appropriate to return for a more concrete discussion?”

## Signals to observe in the reply or meeting

| Positive signal | Caution signal |
| --- | --- |
| Names a current scientific problem, a reading/reproduction task, or a student/project to learn from. | Responds only with a generic “send CV/apply” without research discussion. |
| Distinguishes scientific contribution from maintenance work. | Describes software only as support, with no validation/publication path. |
| Is candid about uncertainty, resources and project status. | Makes broad promises but cannot describe a first milestone. |
| Welcomes a small, concrete pre-project contribution. | Requests substantial unpaid/unclear work before establishing scope and credit. |

No single email reply decides supervision quality. Use it to decide whether to schedule the evidence-gathering meeting described in [meeting-preparation.md](meeting-preparation.md).
